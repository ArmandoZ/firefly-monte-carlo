\begin{block}{Introduction}

The Bayesian approach to probabalistic modeling is appealing it: allows us to
separate modeling assumptions from inference procedures; helps us avoid
overfitting and offers a natural way to control for complexity of models.
However, a full Bayesian approach can often be too computationally
challenging. There are fewer areas where this is more pertient than with
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods, which cannot be practically applied
to large datasets because of the prohobitive cost of updating posteriors when
sampling. For example, when performing Metropolis–Hastings (MH), it is
necessary to evaluate the target posterior density for each proposed
parameter update, and this posterior will usually contain a factor for each
datumThis would then open up MCMC methods to larger datasets than previously
considered feasible.

The posterior likelihood terms for a term

Firefly Monte Carlo seeks to address this problem. It is an auxiliary variable
MCMC algorithm that only queries the likelihoods of a small subset of the
data, at each iteration; not the full set. The auxiliary variable $z_{i}$
can, in a figurative way, be thought of as being like a fire-fly which lights
up (set to 1) thereby determining which likelihoods from the
data-set must be recomputed.

\begin{block}[]

The claim in the paper is that using FLY-MC results in orders of magnitude
faster speed-ups, up to 10 or even 20 fewer likelihood evaluations were
needed to converge to the Markov chain's stationary distribution. In this
presentation we will discuss the FLY-MC framework, our implementations of
FLY-MC and finally, the experiments from the paper that we replicted.

FLYMC's only requirement is that we can compute a lowerbound on the per-datum
likelihoods.

What makes Firefly Monte Carlo unique, in contrast to other metho is that this
new MCMC procedure is exact in the sense that it leaves the true full-data
posterior distribution invariant.FlyMC is a latent variable model which
introduces a collection of Bernoulli variables – one for each datum – with
conditional distributions chosen so that they effectively turn on and off
data points in the posterior, hence the ``firefly'' name. The introduction of
these latent variables does not alter the marginal distribution of the
parameters of interest. Our only requirement is that it be possible to
provide a ``collapsible'' lower bound for each likelihood term. FlyMC can lead
to dramatic performance improvements in MCMC, as measured in wallclock time.
\end{block}


\begin{block}{The Algorithm: Firefly Monte Carlo}

The Firefly Monte Carlo algorithm tackles the problem of sampling from the
posterior distribution of a probabilistic model. We will denote the
parameters of interest as $\theta$ and assume that they have prior
$p(\theta)$. We assume that $N$ data have been observed $\{x_n\}_{n=1}^N$ and
that these data are conditionally independent given $\theta$ under a
likelihood $p(x_n | \theta)$. Our target distribution is therefore
\[
   p(\theta|x_{\mathrm{1:N}})
  \propto p(\theta,x_{\mathrm{1:N}})
  = p(\theta) \prod_{n=1}^N p(x_n | \theta)
\]

For notional convenience, we will write the nth likelihood as a function of
$\theta$ as
\[
  L_{n}(\theta)=p(x_{n}|\theta)
\]

An MCMC sampler makes transitions from a given $\theta$ to a new $\theta'$,
keeping the posterioror distribuion remains invariant. Conventional
algorithms , like Metropoli-Hastings, require the evolution of the
unormalised posterior at full at every iteration. Evaluating all N points
when N is large is the computational bottleneck. FLyMC seeks to address this
problem. For each data point, $x_n$, an auxiliary binary variable $z_{n}\in\{0,1\}$
is added, as well as a function $B_{n}(\theta)$ $0<B_{n}(\theta)\leq L_{n}(\theta)$.
Each $z_{n}$ has the following Bernoulli distribution
conditioned on the parameters:
\[
  p(z_{n}|x_{n},\theta)
  = \left[
      \frac{%
        L_{n}(\theta)-B_{n}(\theta)}{%
        L_{n}(\theta)}
    \right]^{z_{n}}
    \left[
      \frac{%
        B_{n}(\theta)}{%
        L_{n}(\theta)}
    \right]^{1-z_{n}}
\]

We then augment the posterior with the following $N$ variables, for each dstapoint:
\[
  p(\theta,z_{\mathrm{1:N}}|x_{\mathrm{1:N}})
  \propto p(\theta,\{x_{n},z_{n}\}_{\mathrm{n=1:N}})
\]


Augmenting the joint distribution with auxiliary variables does not alter or
damage the original marginal distribution.
